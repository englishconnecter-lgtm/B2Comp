Ranked: The Jobs Americans Quit Most—and Least

B2 ESL lesson using a Visual Capitalist infographic about quit rates, job stability and workplace choices.

Before you read

Some jobs have high turnover: many workers leave voluntarily when they find better pay, better hours, or more stable conditions. This lesson looks at which American industries workers quit most often and which they leave least often.

Warm-up

1. What makes people leave a job voluntarily?

2. Which jobs do you think have the highest turnover?

3. Would you prefer higher pay or stronger job security?

Reading Comprehension

Read the adapted B2 text. Revealed answers appear in green and the exact evidence is highlighted.

Why do some jobs keep workers for many years, while others struggle to keep staff? The answer often depends on pay, benefits, work schedules, promotion opportunities and job security.

The Visual Capitalist graphic uses U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics data from March 2026. Because quits are voluntary, the quit rate shows where workers are most willing to walk away from their jobs.

Accommodation and food services had the highest quit rate, at 4.3%, more than double the U.S. average. Many roles in this sector involve low base pay, changing schedules, physical work and uncertain tips. These conditions can make workers more likely to leave.

Retail trade also had a high quit rate, at 3.1%. Retail jobs often have lower barriers to entry and many part-time positions. As a result, workers may move more easily if they find better pay, hours or working conditions elsewhere.

At the other end of the ranking, government jobs had the lowest quit rates. Federal government jobs had a 0.6% quit rate, while state and local government jobs had a 0.8% rate. These jobs often include pensions, strong benefits, predictable schedules and civil service protections.

Industries with lower pay, less stable schedules or higher turnover tended to have the highest quit rates. By contrast, industries with stronger salaries, benefits and stability, such as information, finance and government, generally had lower quit rates.

The chart also helps explain why the public sector can look very different from the private sector. In many government roles, the value of pensions and benefits increases when employees stay for longer. This gives workers a financial reason not to leave.

Questions

1. What does a quit rate measure?

It measures where workers are most willing to leave their jobs voluntarily.

2. Which industry had the highest quit rate?

Accommodation and food services had the highest quit rate, at 4.3%.

3. Which jobs had the lowest quit rates?

Government jobs had the lowest rates: 0.6% for federal government and 0.8% for state and local government.

4. What kinds of industries tend to have higher quit rates?

Industries with lower pay, less stable schedules or high turnover tend to have higher quit rates.

5. Why might government employees be less likely to leave?

Because pensions and benefits often become more valuable when employees stay longer.
