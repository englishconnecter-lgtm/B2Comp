Mapped: Which Countries Hold the Biggest Mineral Reserves?

B2 ESL lesson using a Visual Capitalist graphic and article information. Focus: reading data, supply chains and natural resources.

Before you read

Modern economies need minerals for buildings, electronics, batteries, defence systems and clean energy. However, the world’s mineral reserves are not evenly distributed. Some countries hold a very large share of important resources.

Reading Comprehension

Read the adapted B2 text. Reveal answers in the white boxes; the exact evidence will be highlighted in the text.

A small group of countries controls the largest reserves of many minerals that are essential for modern economies. These minerals are used in construction, electronics, batteries, fertilisers, nuclear energy, defence systems and renewable technologies.

The Visual Capitalist graphic compares the leading reserve-holder for 18 major minerals. Australia stands out because it has the world’s largest reserves of five resources: gold, uranium, iron ore, zinc and manganese. This gives it one of the most diverse mineral bases in the dataset.

China also has a strategic position. It leads in rare earth elements and graphite, two resources used in technologies such as electric vehicles, wind turbines, semiconductors and defence equipment. Rare earths are especially important because they are difficult to replace in many advanced products.

Some reserves are extremely concentrated. South Africa holds 83% of the world’s platinum-group metal reserves, Morocco controls 69% of phosphate reserves, and the Democratic Republic of Congo accounts for 50% of cobalt reserves. When one country holds such a large share, global supply chains can become more vulnerable.

Other important leaders include Chile for copper and lithium, Indonesia for nickel, Guinea for bauxite and Canada for potash. These patterns show why mineral reserves can create both economic opportunities and geopolitical leverage. Countries with large reserves may attract investment, but other countries may become dependent on them.

Grammar: Passive voice for describing data

Examples from the lesson
Rare earth elements are used in advanced technologies.
The world’s largest phosphate reserves are controlled by Morocco.
Some minerals are concentrated in a single country.

Use the passive voice when the action or situation is more important than the actor, especially in reports, infographics and data commentary.
