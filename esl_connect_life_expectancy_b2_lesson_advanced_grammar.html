How the World Added Decades to Life Expectancy

B2 reading lesson based on a Visual Capitalist chart about life expectancy by World Bank income group from 1960 to 2024.

Before you read

This lesson looks at how average life expectancy has changed in different income groups since 1960. Students practise reading a chart, explaining trends and discussing why people live longer today.

Reading Comprehension

Read the adapted B2 text. Click reveal to show the answer in the white box and highlight the exact evidence.

Life expectancy has increased across every major income group since 1960. In other words, the average person today can expect to live much longer than someone born in the 1960s.

The chart compares life expectancy at birth across four World Bank income groups: high income, upper-middle income, lower-middle income and low income. Although rich countries still have the longest lifespans, many other countries have made major progress.

High-income countries reached 80.3 years in 2024, up from 68.3 years in 1960. This is a gain of 12 years. Because these countries already had relatively long lifespans in 1960, their improvement has been slower but still important.

Upper-middle income countries recorded the fastest improvement, rising from 41.9 years in 1960 to 76.3 years in 2024. That means they added more than 34 years to average life expectancy.

Several changes helped explain this progress. Rising incomes, better sanitation, vaccination programmes, lower child mortality and broader access to healthcare all played a role. These improvements helped many middle-income countries move closer to levels once seen mainly in wealthy countries.

In 1960, people in high-income countries lived about 27 years longer than those in low-income countries; today, the gap is roughly 16 years. This shows that inequality remains, but the difference has narrowed.

Low-income countries have added more than 23 years to average life expectancy since 1960. Much of the world’s longevity progress has therefore come from places that started furthest behind. However, income, healthcare access and living conditions still strongly influence how long people live.

Grammar: Advanced data commentary

Advanced B2 focus: Use more complex structures to describe data accurately and cautiously.

1. Whereas / while to contrast two trends:
High-income countries still lead, whereas upper-middle income countries improved fastest.

2. Having + past participle to show an earlier change before the main point:
Having added more than 34 years, upper-middle income countries recorded the fastest improvement.

3. The more..., the more... to describe a relationship:
The better healthcare becomes, the longer people tend to live.

4. Despite + noun / -ing to show contrast concisely:
Despite major progress, income still influences life expectancy.

Useful language

Describing a trend: The data shows a steady increase in...

Comparing groups: Compared with..., this group...

Explaining causes: This may be due to...

Adding caution: However, the gap has not disappeared completely.
