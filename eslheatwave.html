Reading Comprehension

Read the adapted B2 text. Reveal answers in the white boxes; the exact evidence is highlighted in the text.

NASA says there is strong evidence that Earth is warming at an unprecedented rate. One sign of this change is the number of recent heat records around the world.

Data from Copernicus, the European Earth Observation programme, shows that July 22, 2024 was the world’s hottest daily global average temperature since its records began in 1940. The global average reached 17.16°C, or 62.76°F.

National records are also being broken. Between 2022 and 2025, 41 countries broke or tied their all-time national heat records, and some did so more than once. Japan recorded its hottest day in August, when Isesaki reached 41.8°C.

Other recent records came from many regions. Australia matched its national record in 2022, reaching 50.7°C in Onslow, while Uruguay matched 44.0°C in Florida. In the United Kingdom, temperatures passed the 40°C barrier for the first time in July 2022.

Europe also experienced historic heat. In 2021, Italy recorded 48.8°C in Syracuse, which was reported as the highest temperature ever measured in Europe. France, Belgium and Germany also saw major records during recent heatwaves.

Even Antarctica has recorded unusually high temperatures. In 2020, the Esperanza base reached more than 18°C during the austral summer. However, the official world record remains older: the World Meteorological Organization attributes the world record to Furnace Creek in Death Valley, California, where 56.7°C was reached in 1913.

Questions

1. What broad climate trend does NASA point to?

Earth is warming at an unprecedented rate.

2. What was special about July 22, 2024?

3. How many countries broke or tied their all-time national heat records between 2022 and 2025?

41 countries broke or tied their all-time national heat records.

4. Which European temperature record is mentioned?

Italy recorded 48.8°C in Syracuse, reported as the highest temperature ever measured in Europe.

5. Where and when was the official world temperature record reached?

It was reached at Furnace Creek in Death Valley, California, in 1913.

Grammar: Passive reporting for data and records

Examples
Italy’s 48.8°C record was reported to be the highest temperature ever measured in Europe.
The world record is officially attributed to Furnace Creek.
Many records were broken or tied between 2022 and 2025.

We often use passive structures in data writing when the action or official source is more important than the person doing the action.
