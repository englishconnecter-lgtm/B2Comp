Mapped: Countries With the Most Seniors

B2 lesson on ageing populations, demographic pressure and the economic impact of a growing senior population.

Before you read

This lesson looks at countries where people aged 65 and over make up a large share of the population. Students practise reading a data-based article and talking about ageing societies.

Reading Comprehension

Read the adapted B2 text. Reveal answers in the white boxes and highlight exact evidence in the text.

The world is getting older. In some countries, people aged 65 and over already represent more than a quarter of the population. The Visual Capitalist ranking compares countries by the percentage of residents who are 65 or older, using World Bank data.

Monaco has the highest share of seniors, with 36.17% of residents aged 65 or above. This means that more than one in three people living there are seniors. Monaco is a clear outlier compared with the rest of the ranking.

Japan ranks second, with 29.78% of its population aged 65 or older, making it the oldest major economy in the list. Japan has experienced decades of low birth rates and long life expectancy, which have changed the shape of its population.

Many of the countries near the top are in Europe. Italy, Portugal, Greece, Finland, Germany and Croatia all have more than one in five residents aged 65 or older. Europe dominates the ageing rankings because of below-replacement fertility and rising life expectancy.

The ranking also includes places outside Europe, such as Puerto Rico, Hong Kong and the U.S. Virgin Islands. These examples show that population ageing is not only a European issue, although it is especially concentrated there.

Ageing populations can create economic challenges. When the balance between workers and retirees shifts, a smaller working-age population can slow growth and increase demand for pensions, healthcare and long-term care. Employers may need to keep older workers, use automation or find new ways to deal with labour shortages.

Questions

1. What does the ranking compare?

It compares countries by the percentage of residents aged 65 or older.

2. Which country has the highest share of seniors?

Monaco has the highest share, with 36.17% of residents aged 65 or above.

3. Why is Japan important in the ranking?

Japan ranks second and is the oldest major economy in the list.

4. Why does Europe dominate the ageing rankings?

Because many European countries have below-replacement fertility and rising life expectancy.

5. What economic problems can ageing populations create?

They can slow growth and increase demand for pensions, healthcare and long-term care.
